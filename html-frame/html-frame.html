TV Production in the Age of Coronavirus


An inside look at the setbacks within the entertianment industry as a result of COVID-19.

July 9, 2020


Coronavirus has caused a complete pause in America, affecting the economy and millions of businesses. The pandemic struck the globe by surprise, becoming more significant than anyone could have predicted. Entire industries must shift how they operate, and the unemployment rate has been higher than ever.

In particular, the entertainment industry, including film and TV, took a hard hit. Show production has come to a complete shutdown, causing delays of release dates and uncertainty about the future.



Millions of theatres close across the U.S.



There will be a reduction in the number of people allowed on set


Here is a list of all the shows that have stopped production. According to a Vulture article, many shows rely on their backlog of footage since obtaining new film material isn’t possible. Also, sports broadcasting networks are in trouble. They have been airing old games back to back due to all the cancelations. Other live shows, including newscasts and talk shows, have been filmed live from home.

What will TV shows do when old footage runs out? When will all production come back? There are many unanswered questions about how the entertainment industry will recover from COVID-19, especially since nothing like this has ever happened before.


“The pandemic hit our industry like a ton of bricks and shutdown worldwide production within a matter of days."



Mask with film strip decoration

Susanna Felleman, an executive at HBO, comments on the ongoing struggle in the industry. “The pandemic hit our industry like a ton of bricks and shutdown worldwide production within a matter of days. Now, four months later, the television and motion picture studios are working with the labor unions to create protocols for safety and testing that will enable production to start up again while protecting the health and welfare of all the relevant talent both above and below the line.”



ECONOMIC LOSES


Ampere Analysis estimates that the global loss of growth in the entertainment industry will be $160 billion over 5 years. This loss is across the entertainment industry board, including everything from movies, TV shows, streaming services, Pay TV, and theatrical shows. According to Ampere Analysis, however, the advertising and theatrical industry will suffer the most.



“Theatrical will be hardest hit, set to lose $24.4 billion over
the next five years, with its revenue growth down more than
11 percent over Ampere’s previous forecasts.”


Ampere Analysis writes, “Theatrical will be hardest hit, set to lose $24.4 billion over the next five years, with its revenue growth down more than 11 percent over Ampere’s previous forecasts."



As 6.6 million Americans became unemployed by the end of March, the entertainment had the most job losses, at 37%. of most job cuts.


The film industry has lost 7 billion as of March 2020, and estimates surged to 10 billion (and even 17 billion) between March and May of this year.





The entertainment industry makes up 722,000 jobs in California, and around 10% of employment in all of New York City.
























What about streaming services?


Despite the industry losing billions of dollars, Americans have been watching more TV during this time. However, The New York Times warns that these numbers are not exactly good news.


“With businesses scaling back workers and analysts warning of a recession as global economies slow, a significant number of viewers may decide in the coming months to break away from cable or cut back on streaming subscriptions."


The article writes, "But for media companies, the benefit of having a bigger-than-usual audience may be short-lived as the outbreak threatens to undercut the very structure of their business. With businesses scaling back workers and analysts warning of a recession as global economies slow, a significant number of viewers may decide in the coming months to break away from cable or cut back on streaming subscriptions." 59% of Americans are willing to pay at most $20 for these services. These trends, however, show that streaming services will continue to become the new norm. It is likely that more and more Americans will cancel their cable TV subscription.
